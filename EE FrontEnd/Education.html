Student Debt

More than 45 million Americans collectively owe some $1.6 trillion in outstanding student-loan debt. Democratic candidates broadly agree that student-loan debt is a burden for many families and is a drag on the economy. The federal government already offers student-loan forgiveness programs for public servants and income-based repayment. Democrats’ positions on how to address existing student-loan debt range from tweaking programs meant to help borrowers to cancelling all of the debt.

Teacher Pay

The #RedforEd teacher strike movement ignited in January 2018 with the statewide walkout of West Virginia's teachers. Continuing today, the protests have put a spotlight on inadequate teacher pay and poor classroom conditions. Nearly all the Democratic presidential candidates support raising pay for teachers, but only a handful have offered specifics.

Candidate Candidate Image Stance Political Party
Michael Bennet Boost teacher pay. Democratic
Joe Biden Boost teacher pay. Former Vice President Joe Biden would nearly triple Title I funding and require districts to use the funds to give educators "competitive salaries" and make other "critical investments" before using the money for other purposes. Sen. Elizabeth Warren's plan would quadruple Title I funding and incentivize states to increase pay for teachers, paraprofessionals and other support professionals. Democratic
Pete Buttigieg Boost teacher pay. Democratic
Kamala Harris Boost teacher pay. Sen. Kamala Harris came out early with an aggressive and expansive teacher pay proposal that the California senator’s campaign said would give the average teacher a raise of $13,500. Harris proposes paying for it by strengthening the estate tax and cracking down on loopholes that let the very wealthiest, with estates worth multiple millions or billions of dollars, avoid paying their “fair share,” according to her campaign. Teachers in the highest-need schools, which disproportionately serve students of color, would see their pay rise even further. Democratic
Amy Klobuchar Boost teacher pay. Sen. Amy Klobuchar said she would create a new “Progress Partnerships” program that would provide a federal match to states to increase teacher salaries if states agree to several steps. The steps include creating an “equitable” school infrastructure funding mechanism and convening a commission to review the state’s existing funding formula. Separately, her campaign has said she plans to raise teacher pay as part of her first 100 days in office and that effort would not be contingent on participation in the partnership. Democratic
Bernie Sanders Boost teacher pay. Sen. Bernie Sanders would work with states to set a minimum starting salary for teachers of $60,000. He also says billionaires would pay their "fair share of taxes so that our teachers are not living in poverty." Democratic
Joe Sestak Boost teacher pay. Democratic
Elizabeth Warren Boost teacher pay. Democratic
Cory Booker Give teachers tax credits. Sen. Cory Booker introduced the RAISE Act, S. 2510, designed to make educators eligible for tax credits of at least $1,000 and up to $11,500. Democratic
Julian Castro Boost teacher pay. Former San Antonio Mayor Julian Castro has called for giving teachers a tax credit of up to $10,000. All teachers would get a minimum credit of $2,000. The amount would increase for teachers at schools based on the share of low-income students. Democratic
Steve Bullock Support local districts' efforts. Montana Gov. Steve Bullock's plan, which calls for doubling the federal investment in K-12 education, would include "helping local districts with wage and loan repayment incentives to recruit and retain highly qualified teachers, education professionals, and principals to teach in low-performing or high-poverty school, Title I, principal training, and afterschool programs." Democratic
Marianne Williamson Support local districts' efforts. Marianne Williamson's plan calls for providing "financial support for challenged districts to recruit and retain quality teachers." Democratic
