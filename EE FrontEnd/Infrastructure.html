Infrastrucutre



Investments

Virtually every candidate who has a position on transportation policy believes our roads are congested (and full of potholes), our airports are crammed and our bridges built decades ago need expensive replacements. But where candidates are happy to tell you how they'll spend the money — on broadening highways, on boosting transit, on high speed rail — few have realistic proposals on how to pay for all those new goodies. Not only this but candidates have railed against the poor decisions and infrastructure that led to the water crisis in Flint, Mich. Even then-candidate Donald Trump made a campaign stop there, where he lamented the government failures that created the problem. But then attention quickly faded. It's likely to be an issue again in the 2020 campaign, especially among Democrats eager to burnish their bona fides. The question remains whether any will have substantive ideas for how to fix the problem in Flint — or how to improve aging water and wastewater systems across the country.

Steve Bullock Boost infrastructure spending, but no stated funding mechanism. Boost funding to state and local water systems. Steve Bullock says he wants a $350 billion investment in highway and transit needs, and that he wants to "work with Congress" to find a "new" way to fund infrastructure investments as the nation transitions to cars that burn less gasoline, and therefore generate less revenue from gasoline taxes. He specifically notes a desire to invest in rail, electric buses and bike paths. Steve Bullock has called for fully funding the State Revolving Fund for clean drinking water and eliminating state caps on private activity bonds for water projects, which would bring in $6 to $7 billion per year in private financing, according to his campaign. Democratic
John Delaney Boost infrastructure spending through raising the gasoline tax. A traditionally unpopular political position, so far only one person has come out in favor of raising the gas tax for transportation spending, combined with also changing corporate tax rates: John Delaney. Delaney's proposal is among the most detailed in the Democratic field: a $1 trillion, 10-year plan with a $250 billion federal contribution; the rest would be made up by state and local contributions and public-private partnerships. He also calls for a $50 billion infrastructure bank to fund projects across energy, water, transportation and telecommunications. Boost funding to state and local water systems. John Delaney has called for a $40 billion Water Infrastructure Matching Fund to support state and local government investments in ports, harbors, and aging water systems. Democratic
Julian Castro Boost infrastructure spending, but no stated funding mechanism. Reinstitute the Clean Water Rule. Julian Castro calls for a "climate-conscious infrastructure plan" that would include a $200 billion "Green Infrastructure Fund" that would be used for public transportation, electric vehicle charging stations and other mostly energy-related items. Julian Castro has called for reinstating the Clean Water Rule, as well as fully funding drinking water and clean water State Revolving Funds. Democratic
Elizabeth Warren Pay for infrastructure through changing taxes on corporations, the wealthy. Reinstitute the Clean Water Rule. Elizabeth Warren hasn't expressly called for a large boost in infrastructure investments, but she has said she wants to expand public transit and reduce the climate impact of various modes of transportation. She's also called for making all new vehicles electric by 2030 and requiring electric vehicle charging stations at every highway rest stop. She has said she's open to discussing raising the gasoline tax, which currently fuels most federal transportation programs, but worries it "falls hardest on working people … what I really want to see is, I want to see an ultra-millionaire's tax," she told CNN's Jake Tapper. Elizabeth Warren has called for reinstating the Obama-era clean water rule, which sought to more clearly define which waters are considered under the purview of federal clean water regulations. The Trump administration repealed the rule in September 2019. She also says she wants to make big agribusiness pay for polluting air and water systems by closing loopholes in and beefing up enforcement of federal laws. Democratic
Tom Steyer Create a constitutional right to clean water. Boost infrastructure spending, but no stated funding mechanism Tom Steyer, whose platform is heavily centered on climate change, wants to create a constitutional right to clean water, among other items. Tom Steyer has called for a $2 trillion investment in various types of climate-resilient infrastructure writ-large, including $450 billion in maintenance for roads, bridges and levees, $775 billion for clean transportation initiatives including transit, rail, freight and electric vehicle charging infrastructure. Democratic
Michael Bennet Boost infrastructure spending, but no stated funding mechanism. Michael Bennet has said he will "work to" fix crumbling infrastructure, expand transit and airports, and build out rural broadband. Democratic
Joe Biden Boost infrastructure spending, but no stated funding mechanism. Like other Democratic candidates, Joe Biden has said he supports a large infrastructure investment, but only hints at it within his platform related to energy and building communities that can withstand climate change. He calls for $400 billion over ten years to reduce emissions and the use of fossil fuels that would also create jobs. Democratic
Pete Buttigieg Boost infrastructure spending, but no stated funding mechanism. Pete Buttigieg calls for a "major federal investment" in clean water, transportation, broadband and climate resilience. Democratic
Kamala Harris Boost infrastructure spending, but no stated funding mechanism. Kamala Harris has called for an investment in public transit systems that "that reduce vehicle miles traveled and address gaps in first mile, last mile service." Democratic
Bernie Sanders Boost infrastructure spending, but no stated funding mechanism. Bernie Sanders has said that he supports a "Green New Deal" and wants to build out high speed rail, electric vehicles and public transit in service of reducing emissions. Democratic
Joe Sestak Boost infrastructure spending, but no stated funding mechanism. Joseph Sestak says he wants to increase infrastructure investment by $1 trillion over a decade, create a national infrastructure bank, renew Build America Bonds, and expand the New Markets Tax Credit for underserved communities. Democratic
Marianne Williamson Boost infrastructure spending, but no stated funding mechanism. Marianne Williamson's proposal includes accelerating electric vehicles and their charging infrastructure, rebuilding rail lines, updating clean water systems and "massively investing" in public transit. Democratic
Andrew Yang Boost infrastructure spending, but no stated funding mechanism. Andrew Yang has proposed to spend $1 trillion over five years on unspecified infrastructure. Democratic
Amy Klobuchar Pay for infrastructure by winding down spending on wars and overseas military conflicts. Amy Klobuchar was first out of the gate with an infrastructure proposal she put forward as a centerpiece of her campaign, calling for a $1 trillion investment ($650 billion of which would come from federal funds) in everything from traditional transportation infrastructure to green practices and clean water.
